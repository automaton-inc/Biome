The Great Barrier Reef

Coral Sea, Off the coast of Queensland Australia

Intro

The shallow ocean is an expansive biome with several different ecosystems that vary greatly in regards to average temperature and water; amount of sunlight that penetrates the water, relative distance of the aphotic zone, the nutrients that are found in the water, and the biotic and abiotic factors that are found. The Great Barrier Reef is an example of a shallow ocean aquatic ecosystem. The biotic and abiotic factors of the Great Barrier Reef, which are similar to the abiotic/biotic factors of many other shallow ocean ecosystems can be found in the menu.

Abiotic Factors

FOOD WEB
The Great Barrier Reef borders the East Australian Cordillera mountains and spans from Fraser to Torres Strait of Papua New Guinea. Similar to deep ocean ecosystem, the Great Barrier Reef increases in diameter by one to three centimeters due to tectonic movement. However, although the deep ocean biome increases in depth, the Great Barrier Reef does not pass 150 metres due to the atrophic zone. The Great Barrier Reef is divided into seventy bioregions and thirty of these bioregions are reef biomes. The lagoonal and planar reefs require direct sunlight penetration; the atrophic zone of these bioregions is 150 metres.
The climate of the Great Barrier Reef varies between the North and Southern areas; the South experiences a subtropical climate due to the colder temperatures and moderate-high humidity. The North experiences a tropical climate due to the hotter temperature and extreme humidity. The Great Barrier Reef is made up of calcite particles that forms due to the sedimentation of coral and shell debris (no true soil). The calcite particles are a source of chemosynthesis for many species, including the parrotfish. The Great Barrier Reef varies from 69.8-82.4F with an average salinity of 35 parts/1000. The thirty reef bioregions cover approximately 10% of the reef floor. The annual precipitation of the central regions of the Great Barrier Reef is 11.5-15.9 inches, with the southern and northern tips averaging 42.4-46.8 inches.

Biotic Factors

TROPHIC LEVELS
The primary producers of the Great Barrier Reef ecosystem include the following photosynthetic organisms: plankton, sea weeds, and sea grasses. The primary consumers that belong to the second trophic level include: zooplankton, coral polyps, sponges, mollusks, sea urchins, starfish, and smaller fish. Secondary consumers belonging to the third trophic level include larger reef fish such as trigger fish, parrot fish, surgeon fish, lobsters, and sea turtles. Tertiary consumers of the fourth trophic level include reef sharks which consume secondary consumers. Decomposers that break down dead material include fan worms, sea cucumbers, snails, crabs, bristle worms, and bacteria. Detritivores that consume waste products include brittle starfish, sea cucumbers, and shrimp.
